Method as Position

Every artifact encodes an assumption. The question is whether you name it.

Design Science Research is not neutral. Every artifact encodes an assumption about what counts as a problem, whose constraints matter, and what a solution is for. Naming those assumptions explicitly — and building them into the evaluation criteria — is the difference between method and methodology. Most DSR stops at the artifact. I am interested in the assumptions that precede it.

In multimodal behavioral science, those assumptions are especially consequential: whose data counts, what gets masked and what gets preserved, whose consent model applies, and whether a pipeline built for European gesture corpora can be extended to Yoruba-speaking dyads without reproducing the epistemic exclusions of the infrastructure it was built on. These are design questions. They have right answers — and they have politics.

The utility triad is the framework I use to make those assumptions legible: what signal a pipeline can extract (analytic utility), what each design choice costs in signal quality (reflexive utility), and what can be synthesized — and under what conditions that is epistemically and ethically defensible (generative utility). It extends the Maass et al. (2018, JAIS) theory-/data-driven bridge into the governance demands of AI-era behavioral science.

AU · Analytic Utility

What can a pipeline extract?

Infrastructure for maximising information yield from multimodal corpora. Theory-driven and data-driven as complements. SMASH and EnvisionBOX are the operational testbed.

What does masking cost?

Making de-identification costs legible as a principled design choice, not a binary compliance toggle. MaskBench operationalises this: 88–95% pose quality under blur.

What can be synthesized — and should it?

Synthetic corpora, cross-modal embeddings, governance at the frontier. Highest-opportunity and highest-risk tier. JEGAL and the LEGO collaboration are the live substrate.

The open question

What is the shape of the tradeoff space between analytic power, privacy, and synthesis — and can it be formally characterised across research systems that did not build the infrastructure? That is what the next phase of this programme is for. The tools were built in Western labs on Western data. The unknown unknown is what they assume about the world they were not designed to describe.
